Competitive Markets in the Long Run

Entry, exit, zero economic profit, and the invisible hand.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

A new bubble-tea shop opens and makes great profits. What do you expect to happen on that street over the next year?

Picture the street, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Free entry & exit - profits attract new firms (supply rises, price falls); losses push firms out (supply falls, price rises). Both drive economic profit to zero.
  • Zero economic profit - is a fine, stable place: a normal return for the owner is already counted inside cost. It just means no extra profit luring competitors.

In long-run equilibrium price = minimum average total cost. A demand jump fades as firms enter; a permanent cost rise leaves price permanently higher.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

A competitive market is currently earning positive economic profits. Walk through what happens in the long run, and where price ends up.

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

In a competitive market, firms freely enter when there are profits and exit when there are losses, until economic profit is zero — which means price equals average total cost.
1
Profits attract entry.
New firms join the market.
2
Supply shifts right.
More sellers means the supply curve moves out.
3
Price falls.
As supply grows, the market price drops, shrinking the profits.
4
Stops at zero profit.
Entry continues until price = minimum average total cost and economic profit is zero.
✓ Answer: Firms enter until price falls to minimum average total cost and economic profit is zero.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

Instead, firms are losing money. What happens long run?
Some exit → supply falls → price rises → losses shrink, until economic profit returns to zero.
A permanent rise in an input's cost hits the market. Where does long-run price settle?
Permanently higher. Every firm's average total cost rose, and long-run price equals that — unlike a temporary demand shock, which fades.
