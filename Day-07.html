Competitive Markets in the Long Run

Entry, exit, zero economic profit, and the invisible hand.

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

A new bubble-tea shop opens and makes great profits. What do you expect to happen on that street over the next year?

Picture the street, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Free entry & exit — profits attract new firms (supply rises, price falls); losses push firms out (supply falls, price rises). Both drive economic profit to zero.
  • Zero economic profit — is a stable, fine place: a normal return for the owner is already counted inside cost, so no one is lured in or pushed out.

In long-run equilibrium, price = minimum average total cost. A demand jump fades as firms enter; a permanent cost rise leaves price permanently higher.

♻️ The entry-and-exit engine

🎧 Listen to this section

In a competitive market firms can freely enter and leave. That single fact is an engine that always pushes economic profit back to zero. Watch it run when demand jumps:

Demand rises, so price climbs to P1 and firms earn profit (left). That profit lures new firms in; supply slides right and price drops back to P0 — zero economic profit again (right). Only the quantity is permanently higher. Losses run the same engine in reverse: firms exit, supply falls, price recovers.

⚡ Two shocks, two endings

🎧 Listen to this section

The exam keeps testing the same contrast: a demand shock versus a permanent cost shock. They end in different places.

  • Demand shock (temporary effect on price). Demand rises → short-run profit → entry → price returns to its original min-ATC level. The lasting change is only more firms and more quantity, not a higher price.
  • Permanent input-cost rise (lasting effect on price). Every firm's average total cost is now higher, so long-run price settles permanently higher — because long-run price always equals minimum average total cost, and that floor itself moved up.
Watch what is being asked. After a one-time demand jump, long-run economic profit goes back to zero — so profit "decreases" relative to the elevated short-run level. The market's better technology or trust lifts quantity, not lasting profit.

Worked example · from a past final

A used-car market starts in long-run equilibrium. Then AI makes it far easier for buyers to verify a used car's quality. What happens to the price short run, and to firms' profits in the long run compared with the short run?
1
Easier to trust quality → demand rises.
Short run, price increases and firms earn positive economic profit.
2
Profit attracts entry.
New firms enter; market supply shifts right.
3
Long run, profit returns to zero.
So compared with the elevated short-run profit, long-run profit decreases back to normal.
✓ Answer: price rises in the short run; long-run profit decreases relative to the short run (back to zero economic profit).

Now you try — reveal only after you work it out

A permanent rise in an input's cost hits a competitive market. Where does the long-run price settle?
Permanently higher. Every firm's average total cost rose, and long-run price equals minimum ATC — that floor moved up, so price stays up.
Firms in a competitive market are currently losing money. What happens long run?
Some exit → supply shifts left → price rises → losses shrink, until economic profit is back to zero.

🤝 One more term — the “invisible hand”

Notice what the entry-and-exit engine just did: nobody planned it. Each firm chased its own profit, yet the market landed exactly where output is made at the lowest possible average cost — the most efficient point. That “self-interest leads to an efficient outcome” idea is what Adam Smith called the invisible hand.

Invisible hand: firms and buyers each act in their own self-interest, and market prices guide the market to an efficient outcome — firms end up producing at the most efficient point — with no central planner directing it.

Exam trap: the correct version says owners act on their selfish interests and the market reaches the most efficient point — not that they act selflessly, and not merely “a short-run equilibrium.”
