Start here: scarcity is the engine

You have one evening, one budget, one you. Resources are scarce — limited. Because they're limited, every "yes" to one thing is automatically a "no" to something else. That forced trade-off is why opportunity cost exists at all. No scarcity → no trade-off → no opportunity cost.

One choice, two paths. The cost is the value of the better path you didn't take.

The four things the exam checks

1
It's the alternative, not the price. Opportunity cost is the value of what you gave up — not the money you paid. It can exist even when no money changes hands.
2
It's ONE thing — the single best alternative. Not the sum of everything you skipped. Only the next-best option counts.
3
It's zero only if you can truly do both. If the two options compete for the same time or money, the cost is never zero. "Zero because I enjoy my choice" is always a trap.
4
Watch "None of the answers." On this exam, sometimes the real cost (the given-up alternative) isn't even listed — then "None of the answers" is correct.

See it in two lenses

💼 Founder lens

Friday evening you can push your startup forward, or take a paid freelance gig worth ₪600. You choose the startup.

→ Opportunity cost of the startup work = the ₪600 gig you gave up. (Notice: the cost is the alternative — here it happens to carry a price tag — not money you spent.)

☕ Everyday lens

Sunday morning you can study for the exam, or sleep one more hour. You study.

→ Opportunity cost of studying = that hour of sleep. No money changed hands — still a real cost.

👑 The "zero" case

On your walk you can listen to a podcast and walk the dog at the same time — they don't compete for the same time.

→ Opportunity cost of listening ≈ zero, because you truly do both at once.

Two classic trap answers: "the price/ticket you paid" (that's a cost, but not the opportunity cost) and "zero because you enjoy it more" (enjoying a choice never makes its cost zero).
