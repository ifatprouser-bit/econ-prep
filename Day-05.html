⏸ Before you listen — guess first

Decide, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Total & average — Total cost = fixed + variable. ATC = total cost ÷ quantity. AVC = variable cost ÷ quantity.
  • Marginal cost — the extra cost of one more unit. Rebuild variable cost by ADDING each marginal cost, row by row — never subtract.

Average total cost is U-shaped: it falls as fixed cost spreads over more units, then rises; marginal cost crosses it at its lowest point.

🧾 The six cost columns — and how to fill them

🎧 Listen to this section

The exam hands you a half-empty cost table — Ms. H's tea — and asks you to fill it in. Don't let the six short names scare you: every column comes from just two facts you're given — the fixed cost, and the marginal cost of each box. Here is exactly what each name means, in Ms. H's tea, with its value at 3 boxes so you can spot it in the full table below.

NameWhat it is — in Ms. H's teaFormulaAt 3 boxes
FC — Fixed CostThe cost that never changes — her equipment and space. She pays it even if she makes 0 boxes.given200
VC — Variable CostThe costs that grow with each box she makes — tea leaves, packaging. Built by adding up the marginal costs.add the MCs60
TC — Total CostEverything she spends, all in.FC + VC260
MC — Marginal CostThe extra cost of making one more box — the jump in total cost.∆TC / ∆Q30
ATC — Average Total Cost (also written AC)Cost per box, with the fixed cost included.TC / Q86.7
AVC — Average Variable CostCost per box, with the fixed cost left out.VC / Q20

ATC and AVC are the same idea — cost per box — differing by one thing only: ATC carries the fixed cost, AVC doesn't. That difference has its own name on your formula sheet: AFC — Average Fixed Cost = FC ÷ Q, the slice of fixed cost each box has to carry.

The move is the same every row: turn the one clue you're given back into that row's variable cost (or total cost) — then the rest of the row falls out from the definitions. And fixed cost never changes, so it's 200 on every row.
1
Fixed cost first, then the zero row.
FC is the same everywhere → write 200 down the whole FC column. At 0 boxes there's no variable cost, so VC = 0, TC = 200.
2
Box 1 — you're given VC = 20.
TC = 200 + 20 = 220 · MC = 220 − 200 = 20 · ATC = 220 ÷ 1 = 220 · AVC = 20 ÷ 1 = 20
3
Box 2 — you're given MC = 10.
Add it to the previous VC: VC = 20 + 10 = 30 · TC = 230 · ATC = 230 ÷ 2 = 115 · AVC = 30 ÷ 2 = 15
4
Box 3 — you're given AVC = 20. Run it backwards.
VC = AVC × Q = 20 × 3 = 60 · MC = 60 − 30 = 30 · TC = 260 · ATC = 260 ÷ 3 ≈ 86.7
5
Box 4 — you're given ATC = 80. Run it backwards too.
TC = ATC × Q = 80 × 4 = 320 · VC = 320 − 200 = 120 · MC = 320 − 260 = 60 · AVC = 120 ÷ 4 = 30
6
Box 5 — you're given AVC = 42.
VC = 42 × 5 = 210 · TC = 410 · MC = 410 − 320 = 90 · ATC = 410 ÷ 5 = 82

Read one row to see it click. Box 3: VC = 20 + 10 + 30 = 60 (the marginal costs added). TC = 200 + 60 = 260. ATC = 260 ÷ 3 ≈ 86.7. AVC = 60 ÷ 3 = 20. The MC of box 3 is 30 — the jump in TC from 230 to 260.

The trap: to get the next VC you add the marginal cost to the previous VC — you never subtract. Costs only climb as you make more.

📈 What the cost curves look like

🎧 Listen to this section

Now plot three of those per-box costs — ATC, AVC and MC — as Ms. H makes more boxes. A clean, memorable shape appears.

ATC (purple) and AVC (green) are U-shaped; MC (red) rises and cuts through the bottom of each U.

Why the U-shape? At first, Ms. H spreads her fixed 200 over very few boxes, so cost-per-box starts high — and it falls quickly as she makes more (the fixed cost splits into thinner slices). But push too hard and each extra box gets costlier to make (MC climbs), which pulls the average back up. Falling, then rising — a U.

Why does MC cut through the lowest point of ATC and AVC? Think of your GPA. MC is your next box's cost — like your next quiz score; ATC is the running average — your GPA. A quiz below your GPA drags it down; a quiz above pulls it up. So your GPA bottoms out exactly where the next score crosses it. Same here: while MC is below an average, that average falls; once MC rises above, the average climbs. They meet at the average's minimum.

Why does the gap between ATC and AVC shrink? That gap is the average fixed cost, AFC = FC ÷ Q — the fixed 200 divided among the boxes. Make more boxes and 200 splits into smaller pieces, so the two curves squeeze together.

Worth remembering: the bottom of the ATC curve — where MC = ATC — is the price a competitive market settles at in the long run (P = MC = ATC). You'll use that on Days 6–7.

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

Variable cost = the marginal costs ADDED. Total cost = fixed + variable. ATC = total ÷ quantity.
1
Add the marginal costs → variable cost.
20 + 10 + 30 = 60
2
Add fixed cost → total cost.
200 + 60 = 260
3
ATC = total ÷ quantity.
260 ÷ 3 ≈ 86.7
✓ Answer: total cost at 3 cakes = 260; average total cost ≈ 86.7.

Now you try — reveal only after you work it out

Using the same table above (fixed cost 200; marginal costs 20, 10, 30), what is the average VARIABLE cost at 3 cakes?
20. Variable cost is 60; AVC = 60 ÷ 3 = 20 (fixed cost is left out).
The 4th cake raises total cost from 260 to 320. What is the marginal cost of the 4th cake?
60. Marginal cost = the change in total cost = 320 − 260 = 60.
