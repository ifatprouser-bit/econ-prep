Production & Costs — the Cost Table

Fill the table without the classic add-versus-subtract slip.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

A bakery pays fixed rent no matter what; flour and labour grow with each cake. If a table gave you the marginal cost of each cake, would you ADD them or subtract them to rebuild total cost?

Decide, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Total, average - Total cost = fixed + variable. ATC = total cost ÷ quantity. AVC = variable cost ÷ quantity.
  • Marginal cost - the extra cost of one more unit. Build variable cost by ADDING each marginal cost, row by row — never subtract.

Average total cost is U-shaped: it falls as fixed cost spreads, then rises; marginal cost crosses it at its lowest point.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

A bakery's fixed cost is 200. The marginal cost of the 1st cake is 20, of the 2nd is 10, and of the 3rd is 30. What is the total cost of making 3 cakes, and the average total cost at 3 cakes?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

Variable cost = the marginal costs ADDED up. Total cost = fixed + variable. ATC = total cost ÷ quantity.
1
Add the marginal costs to get variable cost.
20 + 10 + 30 = 60
2
Add fixed cost.
200 + 60 = 260 total cost
3
Average total cost = total ÷ quantity.
260 ÷ 3 ≈ 86.7
4
Done.
Notice costs climb as output rises — that is why you add, never subtract.
✓ Answer: Total cost at 3 cakes = 260; average total cost ≈ 86.7.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

Using the same table, what is the average VARIABLE cost at 3 cakes?
20. Variable cost is 60; AVC = 60 ÷ 3 = 20 (fixed cost is left out).
The 4th cake raises total cost from 260 to 320. What is the marginal cost of the 4th cake?
60. Marginal cost = the change in total cost = 320 − 260 = 60.
