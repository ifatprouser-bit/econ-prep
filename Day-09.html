Macro 1 — GDP

What counts in GDP, and nominal versus real versus per capita.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

A country reports its economy 'grew 5 percent in dollars' this year. But prices also rose. Did it actually produce more — or just charge more?

Answer, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Nominal GDP - current prices × current quantities — it mixes together real output and price changes.
  • Real GDP - base-year prices × current quantities — it strips out inflation. Real GDP ÷ population = standard of living.

Only current production counts. Used and second-hand goods add nothing to GDP — they were counted when first made.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

A country makes only tyres and batteries. This year, tyres sell for 185 each with 120 made, and batteries sell for 105 each with 310 made. What is this year's nominal GDP?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

Nominal GDP = add up (price × quantity) for every good at this year's prices.
1
Tyres.
185 × 120 = 22,200
2
Batteries.
105 × 310 = 32,550
3
Add them.
22,200 + 32,550 = 54,750
4
That is nominal GDP.
Real GDP would redo this with a base year's prices instead.
✓ Answer: Nominal GDP = 54,750.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

A country's total real GDP is 54,750 and its population is 9. What is its real GDP per capita, and what does that measure?
About 6,083 (54,750 ÷ 9). 'Per capita' means 'per person', so real GDP per capita = real GDP ÷ population. You divide once — it is the standard-of-living measure, output per person.
The country re-sells 1,000 used engines abroad. Effect on GDP?
No impact. Used goods were counted when first produced; GDP counts only current production.
