Macro 1 — GDP

What counts in GDP, and nominal versus real versus per capita.

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

A country reports its economy "grew 5 percent in dollars" this year. But prices also rose. Did it actually produce more — or just charge more?

Answer, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Nominal GDP — current prices × current quantities; it mixes real output with price changes.
  • Real GDP — base-year prices × current quantities; it strips out inflation. Real GDP ÷ population = standard of living.

Only current production counts. Used and second-hand goods add nothing to GDP — they were counted when first made.

🌐 Nominal vs real vs per capita

🎧 Listen to this section

All three measures multiply prices by quantities. The only differences are which prices you use and whether you divide by population.

Nominal GDP = Σ (this year's price × this year's quantity). Real GDP = Σ (BASE year's price × this year's quantity). Real GDP per capita = real GDP ÷ population — the standard-of-living measure.

Why two versions? Because nominal can rise just from higher prices. Real GDP freezes prices at a base year so any change is pure output. Watch how they can split apart:

From 2026 to 2027 nominal GDP nudges up while real GDP actually falls — prices rose enough to mask a drop in real output. That's exactly why we judge growth by real GDP, not nominal.

1

2

✓ Real GDP 2027 (base 2026) = 102,625. Since real GDP 2026 = 103,230, real GDP fell — a change of about −0.59%.

Now you try — reveal only after you work it out

In which year is the standard of living highest? (Use real GDP per capita, base 2026.)
2027. Real GDP ÷ population: 2025 = 98,000 ÷ 10 = 9,800; 2026 = 103,230 ÷ 11 ≈ 9,385; 2027 = 102,625 ÷ 9 ≈ 11,403. The smaller population makes 2027 the highest per person.
Crunchville re-sells 1,000 used engines abroad. Effect on GDP?
No impact. Used goods were counted when first produced; GDP counts only current production.
