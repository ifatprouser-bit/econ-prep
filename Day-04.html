Subsidies & Shifts in Equilibrium

When curves move — and the exam's favourite answer, 'it cannot be known'.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

The government starts paying bakers a little for every loaf they sell. Before any economics — do you think bread gets cheaper or pricier, and do we get more of it or less?

Guess both, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Subsidy to sellers - shifts supply RIGHT → price falls, quantity rises (the government encourages it, so we get more, cheaper).
  • Two curves shift oppositely - one of price/quantity is pinned down; the other 'cannot be known' without the sizes of the shifts.

Take each shift one at a time and compare the arrows: where they agree you know the answer; where they disagree, it cannot be known.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

At the same time, two things happen in the bread market: buyers' incomes rise (demand shifts right) AND a tax on bakers raises their costs (supply shifts left). What happens to the price of bread, and to the quantity sold?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

Handle one shift at a time. Note its effect on price and on quantity. Then compare: agreement = known; disagreement = cannot be known.
1
Demand shifts right.
On its own: price up, quantity up.
2
Supply shifts left.
On its own: price up, quantity down.
3
Compare price.
Both push price UP → price definitely rises.
4
Compare quantity.
One pushes up, one pushes down → the change in quantity cannot be known.
✓ Answer: Price definitely rises; the change in quantity cannot be known.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

Only a subsidy is given to sellers (supply shifts right, demand unchanged). Price and quantity?
Price falls, quantity rises. One curve only → both directions are known.
A rule forces sellers to disclose something unappealing, lowering demand (supply unchanged). Price and quantity?
Both fall. Demand shifts left along a fixed supply curve.
