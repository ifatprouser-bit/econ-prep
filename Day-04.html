Subsidies & Shifts in Equilibrium

Reading any change in the market — and the exam's favourite answer, "it cannot be known."

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

The government starts paying bakers a little for every loaf they sell. Before any economics — does bread get cheaper or pricier, and do we get more of it or less?

Guess both, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • A subsidy to sellers shifts supply RIGHT → price falls, quantity rises.
  • A tax / higher cost on sellers shifts supply LEFT → price rises, quantity falls.

When TWO curves move at once and they pull a variable in opposite directions, that variable "cannot be known" without the sizes of the shifts.

🎯 Read any single shift in two moves

🎧 Listen to this section

Every "if X happens, what happens to price and quantity?" question is really two small questions: which curve moves, and which way? Then you read the result off one table.

Move 1 — name the curve. Anything that changes sellers' costs or numbers moves SUPPLY. Anything that changes buyers' wants (income, tastes, related prices, number of buyers) moves DEMAND.
Move 2 — name the direction. More / easier-cheaper → RIGHT. Less / harder-costlier → LEFT.
What shiftsPriceQuantity
Demand → right↑↑
Demand → left↓↓
Supply → right (subsidy, lower cost, new tech, more sellers)↓↑
Supply → left (tax, higher input cost, fewer sellers)↑↓

A subsidy, on the graph

The subsidy makes selling cheaper, so supply slides right to S'. The new crossing sits lower and further out — cheaper bread, more of it. A tax does the mirror image: supply slides left, price up, quantity down.

⚖️ When TWO curves move — "cannot be known"

🎧 Listen to this section

Sometimes two things change together. The method is the same — just do each shift on its own, write its two arrows, then compare. Where the arrows agree, you know the answer. Where they disagree, that variable cannot be known without the sizes of the shifts. That phrase is a real exam option, and it's correct surprisingly often.

Demand right and supply left both push price UP — so price is known. But demand right pushes quantity right while supply left pushes it back — so the new quantity could land left, on top of, or right of the old one. Quantity "cannot be known."

Worked example · do each shift, then compare

At the same time: buyers' incomes rise (demand shifts right) AND a new tax on bakers raises their costs (supply shifts left). What happens to the price of bread and the quantity sold?
1
Demand → right, on its own.
Price up, quantity up.
2
Supply → left, on its own.
Price up, quantity down.
3
Compare the arrows.
Price: up and up → known, price rises. Quantity: up and down → cannot be known.
✓ Answer: price definitely rises; the change in quantity cannot be known.

Now you try — reveal only after you work it out

Only a subsidy is given to sellers (supply right, demand unchanged). Price and quantity?
Price falls, quantity rises. One curve only → both directions are known.
A good becomes more popular (demand right) AND new technology lowers sellers' costs (supply right). Price and quantity?
Quantity definitely rises (both push it right). Price cannot be known — demand-right lifts it, supply-right lowers it.
