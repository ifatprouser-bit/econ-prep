🛒 Inflation and the CPI

🎧 Listen to this section

The Consumer Price Index tracks what one fixed basket of goods costs over time, compared to a base year.

CPI = (cost of the basket this year ÷ cost in the base year) × 100. The base year is always 100. Inflation = the percent change in the CPI from one year to the next.

Worked example · from a past final

Crunchville's CPI basket is 4 tyres + 1 battery, base year 2026. Tyre/battery prices: 2025 = 180/100, 2026 = 185/105, 2027 = 190/102. What is the inflation rate in 2027?
1
Cost of the basket each year.
2026: 4×185 + 105 = 845 2027: 4×190 + 102 = 862
2
CPI (base 2026 = 100).
CPI 2027 = 862 ÷ 845 × 100 ≈ 102.0
3
Inflation = % change in CPI.
(102.0 − 100) ÷ 100 ≈ 2.0% inflation
✓ Inflation in 2027 ≈ 2% (prices rose, so it's inflation, not deflation).

👥 The labour force & unemployment

🎧 Listen to this section

The single biggest trap here is the denominator. You divide by the labour force, not by everyone.

The two people "not in the labour force" (retired, students, not looking) are counted in neither the labour force nor as unemployed. The unemployment rate is 1 ÷ 6, never 1 ÷ 8.

🏭 The two policy levers

🎧 Listen to this section

Government has fiscal policy (spending and taxes); the central bank has monetary policy (interest rates). Each can be expansionary (lift demand) or contractionary (cool demand).

Expansionary — for a recessionContractionary — for high inflation
Fiscal (govt)More spending, lower taxesLess spending, higher taxes
Monetary (central bank)Lower interest ratesHigher interest rates
Match the medicine to the illness. Recession (weak demand) → expansionary on both levers. High inflation (overheated demand) → contractionary on both. Contractionary monetary policy's goal is to reduce aggregate demand to bring inflation down.
