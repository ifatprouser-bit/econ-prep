Price Elasticity of Demand

How sensitive buyers are to price — and what it does to revenue.

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

Two shops each raise prices 10 percent. A trendy boba shop loses half its customers; the only pharmacy in town loses almost none. Which one should think hard before raising prices?

Answer, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Elastic (size of elasticity above 1) — quantity reacts a lot (many substitutes). Raising the price LOWERS total revenue.
  • Inelastic (size below 1) — quantity barely reacts (a necessity, few substitutes). Raising the price RAISES total revenue.

The working line: % change in quantity = elasticity × % change in price. Revenue follows from elasticity — but profit also needs costs.

🧮 The formula, and how to use it

🎧 Listen to this section

Elasticity is just one ratio: how much quantity moves for a given price move.

elasticity = (% change in quantity) ÷ (% change in price). Rearranged for calculations: % change in quantity = elasticity × % change in price.

Worked example · from a past final

Lavender sells erasers at 10 shekels and sells 20 a month. The price elasticity of her erasers is −2.5. She raises the price to 12. How many erasers will she sell?
1
% change in price.
10 → 12 is +20%
2
Apply elasticity → % change in quantity.
−2.5 × 20% = −50%
3
New quantity.
20 × (1 − 0.50) = 10 erasers
✓ Answer: she sells 10 erasers a month.

Now you try — reveal only after you work it out

Coffee is 4 dollars, 4,000 cups sold, elasticity −1.5. The price rises to 5. How many cups now?
2,500 cups. Price 4 → 5 is +25%. −1.5 × 25% = −37.5%. 4,000 × (1 − 0.375) = 2,500.
Blankets are 200, 50 sold, elasticity −0.8. Price rises to 220. How many now?
46 blankets. Price 200 → 220 is +10%. −0.8 × 10% = −8%. 50 × (1 − 0.08) = 46.

📏 Finding elasticity from two points — the midpoint method

Sometimes the exam doesn't hand you the elasticity — it gives two prices with their quantities and asks you to compute it. Use the average of the two prices and the two quantities as the base, so you get the same answer whichever way the price moves.

elasticity = (change in quantity ÷ average quantity) ÷ (change in price ÷ average price).

Worked example

At a price of 5, a café sells 100 coffees a day; at a price of 7, it sells 60. What is the price elasticity of demand between these two prices?
1
% change in quantity (over the average quantity).
(60 − 100) ÷ ((100 + 60) / 2) = −40 ÷ 80 = −50%
2
% change in price (over the average price).
(7 − 5) ÷ ((5 + 7) / 2) = 2 ÷ 6 ≈ +33.3%
3
Divide.
−50% ÷ 33.3% ≈ −1.5
✓ Elasticity ≈ −1.5 — bigger than 1 in size, so demand is elastic here.

If the question lists two groups of buyers, first add their quantities at each price, then put the combined totals through this same formula.

📊 Elastic vs inelastic — and what it does to revenue

🎧 Listen to this section

The size of the number tells you the shape of the curve and the fate of revenue.

Same price change, two very different reactions. A steep curve (inelastic, a necessity) barely budges in quantity; a flat curve (elastic, easily substituted) swings a lot. That's why a pharmacy can raise prices and a trendy café cannot.
Revenue rule. Elastic (|e| > 1): price and revenue move OPPOSITE ways — raise the price, revenue falls. Inelastic (|e| < 1): price and revenue move TOGETHER — raise the price, revenue rises. And revenue alone never tells you profit — you'd also need to know how costs changed.
