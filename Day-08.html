Price Elasticity of Demand

How sensitive buyers are to price — and what it does to revenue.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

Two shops each raise prices 10 percent. A trendy boba shop loses half its customers; the only pharmacy in town loses almost none. Which one should think hard before raising prices?

Answer, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Elastic (size of elasticity above 1) - quantity reacts a lot (lots of substitutes). Raising price LOWERS total revenue.
  • Inelastic (size below 1) - quantity barely reacts (a necessity, few substitutes). Raising price RAISES total revenue.

The working line: % change in quantity = elasticity × % change in price. Revenue follows from elasticity, but profit also needs costs.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

A seller charges 10 and sells 20 units a month. The price elasticity of demand is −2.5. She raises the price to 12. How many units will she sell, and does her revenue rise or fall?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

% change in quantity = elasticity × % change in price. Then revenue = price × quantity.
1
Find the % change in price.
10 → 12 is +20%
2
Apply elasticity for the % change in quantity.
−2.5 × 20% = −50%
3
New quantity.
20 × (1 − 0.5) = 10 units
4
Compare revenue.
old 10×20 = 200 → new 12×10 = 120 (falls)
✓ Answer: She sells 10 units, and revenue falls from 200 to 120 (demand is elastic).

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

Demand is inelastic and a seller raises the price. What happens to revenue?
It rises. Quantity falls by a smaller % than the price rises, so price × quantity goes up.
Price rises 10% and quantity falls 4%. Is demand elastic or inelastic?
Inelastic. Quantity moved less than price (size of elasticity = 0.4, below 1).
