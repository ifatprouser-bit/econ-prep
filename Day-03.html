Price Ceilings & Floors

What happens when the government freezes a price — and who gains, who loses.

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

A city rules that no store may sell Bamba above a very low price — lower than the normal price. What happens to the Bamba on the shelves?

Take 20 seconds. Picture the shelf. Guess — then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Ceiling — a legal MAX price. Bites only when set BELOW the normal price → a shortage (empty shelves).
  • Floor — a legal MIN price. Bites only when set ABOVE the normal price → a surplus (overflowing shelves).

The amount actually traded is always the short side — the smaller of what buyers want and what sellers offer at the controlled price.

🏭 How a price control bends the market

🎧 Listen to this section

A price control is the government pinning the price at a level the market wouldn't choose. There are two kinds, and the trick is the same for both: compare the controlled price to the normal (equilibrium) price, then read the gap.

Look at the two diagrams

Left: the ceiling sits below the crossing, so sellers offer little (Qs) but buyers want lots (Qd) — the gap is a shortage. Right: the floor sits above the crossing, so sellers offer lots (Qs) but buyers want little (Qd) — the gap is a surplus.

The short-side rule (this is what they ask)

Only the smaller side can actually trade. Under a binding ceiling, sellers are the short side → quantity sold = quantity supplied at the capped price. Under a binding floor, buyers are the short side → quantity sold = quantity demanded at the propped-up price.

Who gains and who loses

Under a ceiling below the normal price: some consumers gain — the lucky ones who grab the good at the low price — but others find empty shelves, and all sellers lose (lower price and lower quantity). That exact phrasing — "some consumers benefit, all sellers lose" — is a repeat exam answer.

A control that doesn't cross the market does nothing: a ceiling above the normal price, or a floor below it, is not binding — price and quantity don't change.

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

A ceiling below the normal price causes a shortage → sellers are the short side → amount sold = total quantity supplied at the capped price.
1
Read one seller's supply at the 50 cap.
From the table, each seller supplies 96 cakes.
2
Count the sellers.
There are 20 sellers.
3
Multiply — sellers are the short side.
96 × 20 = 1,920 cakes.
✓ Answer: 1,920 cakes. Buyers want more, but only what sellers make at the cap can change hands.

Normal price is 10 (supply 18×10 = 180 = demand 12×15 = 180). The 15 floor is above 10 → binding → buyers are the short side. Sold = demand at 15 = 8 × 15 = 120 units.
The mayor sets a ceiling ABOVE the normal price. Before the rule, 500 units sold. How many sell now?
500 — no change. A ceiling above the normal price is not binding.
