Price Ceilings & Floors

What happens when the government freezes a price — and who gains, who loses.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

A city rules that no store may sell Bamba above a very low price — lower than the normal price. What happens to the Bamba on the shelves?

Take 20 seconds. Picture the shelf. Guess — then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Shortage - buyers want more than is supplied. Caused by a price ceiling set BELOW the normal price (empty shelves).
  • Surplus - more is supplied than buyers want. Caused by a price floor set ABOVE the normal price (overflowing shelves).

The amount actually traded is always the short side — the side that gives way — because the market balances itself there.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

In town T the market for chocolate cake is competitive. There are 20 sellers. The mayor sets a maximum price (a ceiling) of 50 shekels per cake — below the normal market price. At a price of 50, each seller is willing to supply 96 cakes, while buyers want far more. How many cakes are actually sold?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

When a ceiling is below the normal price there is a shortage, so the amount sold is the short side = the quantity supplied at the capped price.
1
Per-seller supply at the capped price.
Each seller supplies 96 cakes.
2
Count the sellers.
There are 20 sellers.
3
Multiply.
96 × 20 = 1,920 cakes
4
That is the answer, because sellers are the short side.
Buyers want more, but only what sellers make can be sold.
✓ Answer: 1,920 cakes are sold.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

A market has 15 sellers. Under a binding ceiling each seller supplies 40 units, and buyers want far more. How many units are sold?
600 units. Short side = 40 × 15 = 600.
The mayor sets a ceiling ABOVE the normal price. Before the rule, 500 units sold. How many sell now?
500 — no change. A ceiling above the normal price is not binding.
