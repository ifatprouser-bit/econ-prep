# How to build podcasts that actually teach — the research

A research brief for Ifat. The short version: your instinct is correct, and the learning science explains exactly *why*. Audio is great for principles, stories, and intuition. It is one of the *worst* formats for calculations. Below is what the evidence says, and how to redesign around it.

---

## 1. The core finding: audio is "transient," and that breaks math

When you hear a sentence, it is gone the instant it is spoken. Researchers call this **transient information**. Your working memory has to hold each spoken piece *and* connect it to the next, with nothing to look back at. Written information, by contrast, is **permanent** — your eye can re-read the previous line for free.

This is the **transient information effect** from Cognitive Load Theory: for complex material, listening is *harder* than reading, because audio forces you to keep everything in your head at once. The research finding is blunt — *"Reading is superior to listening when dealing with complex expository information due to the transient nature of audio."* And critically: **short** audio works, but **long** audio overloads, *"because of the abundance of transient information in the longer, auditory piece."*

A calculation is the worst possible case for this. "Take 185 times 120, plus 240 times 202, plus 105 times 310…" — by the time the third term is spoken, the first has evaporated, and there is no number on screen to anchor to. You felt this directly. It is not you; it is the medium.

**Takeaway:** Use audio for ideas that survive being spoken once. Move anything you'd need to *hold, see, or re-check* (numbers, tables, multi-step formulas) out of audio and into a visual, practice format.

---

## 2. The split: what to teach by listening vs. by doing

| Teach by **listening** (audio/podcast) | Teach by **doing** (practice/visual) |
|---|---|
| Principles & intuitions ("why a ceiling makes a shortage") | Calculations (GDP, CPI, elasticity, cost tables) |
| The *logic* of a relationship ("low price → buyers want more, sellers offer less") | Step-by-step worked examples you read and follow |
| Stories & real examples that anchor a concept | Anything with a table, graph, or formula to track |
| Definitions and the "shape" of a topic | Retrieval practice (quizzes, solving problems) |
| The *meaning* of a result ("what a –0.59% change tells us") | Drilling the *procedure* that produces that number |

The reason the right column must be *done*, not *heard*, is the **worked-example effect**: novices learn procedures far better by studying a written, step-by-step solution than by hearing it or solving blind — because the steps stay on the page and don't overload working memory. (Note: this helps *beginners* most; once you're fluent, you switch to solving yourself — the "expertise reversal effect.")

**Implication for our course:** the podcast should carry the *principle*; a paired visual "How to calculate" card should carry the *math*. Exactly what you proposed.

---

## 3. Five design rules for a learning podcast (all evidence-backed)

### Rule 1 — Lead with a question, then pause (the *pretesting effect*)
Asking a question *before* giving the answer makes the answer stick better — even when the learner guesses wrong. This is the **pretesting / prequestion effect**: *"taking a test before learning new information leads to stronger memory."* It works whether you answer out loud or just think it ("covert" works as well as "overt"), and it *"reduced mind wandering and improved later learning."*

> So a good segment opens: *"A city caps the price of bread below the normal price. What do you think happens to the shelves? … Pause the audio and guess."* Then, after a real silence, the explanation lands on prepared ground.

One caveat the research is honest about: the benefit is **specific to what you asked**. So the pre-question has to point at the exact idea you want remembered.

### Rule 2 — Build in real pauses (the *pause procedure*)
In classic studies, lecturers who **paused ~3 times for 2 minutes** so students could think or recall produced *significantly* higher recall and test scores; 83.6% of students said it helped. Silence is not dead air — it is when memory consolidates. A learning podcast should literally *tell you to stop and think*, and leave the gap.

### Rule 3 — Wrap every principle in a story (the *narrative effect*)
This is your strongest intuition, and the evidence is overwhelming. A meta-analysis of **75+ samples and 33,000+ people** found stories are *"more easily understood and better recalled than essays"* — narrative text is remembered roughly **twice as well** as expository text. Stories work because they connect new ideas to things you already know and carry a little emotion, which deepens processing.

> Don't say: *"A binding ceiling creates excess demand."* Say: *"Remember Bamba flying off the shelves before a holiday. Now the government says no one may raise the price. Everyone rushes to buy the cheap Bamba — but the makers don't find it worth producing more. Empty shelves by noon. That empty shelf is a shortage."* Same principle, but now it has a picture and a feeling.

Use the course's own characters — the chocolate-cake town, the tea-seller, Bamba — so the story and the exam question share a world.

### Rule 4 — One idea per segment (the *segmenting principle*)
People learn better from material *"presented in user-paced segments rather than as a continuous unit."* Your current podcasts pack 6–8 ideas into one 9-minute stream with no breaks — that's the overload you felt. Better: short segments (one principle each), with a clear stop between them, so *you* control the pace.

### Rule 5 — Name the pieces first (the *pretraining principle*)
People learn a process better when they *"know the names and characteristics of the main concepts"* beforehand. So a 20-second intro that just names the players — *"three words today: ceiling, shortage, surplus"* — frees up your mind to follow the actual reasoning instead of decoding vocabulary mid-stream.

---

## 4. What carries the learning *after* listening

Two more findings shape the *non*-audio half of the system:

- **Retrieval practice (the testing effect):** recalling beats re-reading. Roediger & Karpicke found learners who *practiced recall* kept **61%** after a week vs. **40%** for re-readers. → This is why each day needs a quiz, and why the quiz matters as much as the podcast.
- **Spacing (distributed practice):** the same study time spread across days beats one long session — shown across **200+ studies**, including math. → Short daily sessions (your ~30-min plan) is already the right shape; we just keep revisiting earlier topics, not only the new one.

---

## 5. The redesign this points to

For each topic, split into two paired pieces:

1. **A short "Principle" podcast (~4–6 min, one idea):**
   opens with a pre-question and a pause → tells 2–3 stories from the course world → states the principle plainly → ends with "pause and recall the one idea." No numbers read aloud.

2. **A "How to calculate" visual card (read, not heard):**
   the formula, then **one fully worked example** laid out step by step, then a couple to try yourself with instant feedback. This is where the math lives.

The quiz stays as the retrieval step, and the calendar keeps topics spaced.

This directly fixes your three complaints: too dense (→ one idea per segment), too fast for math (→ math leaves audio entirely), and no room to think (→ pre-questions + real pauses + stories).

---

## 6. Seed for a future "learning-podcast builder" skill

If we turn this into a reusable skill, its rules would be:
- Split content into *principle* (audio) vs. *procedure* (visual) before writing a word.
- Never read multi-step numbers aloud.
- Every segment = one idea, opened by a pre-question + pause, carried by ≥2 stories, closed by a recall prompt.
- Keep segments short and user-paced; name key terms up front.
- Pair every podcast with a worked-example card and a retrieval quiz; space topics over days.
