Short-Run Supply & the Shutdown Decision

Two different questions: should I produce right now, and should I stay in the market at all.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

A food truck is losing money this month. Its pitch fee is already paid, whatever it does. Should it open tomorrow, or stay shut?

Decide, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Shutdown test (short run) - produce now if price ≥ average variable cost. Fixed costs are already spent, so cover the variable cost and a bit more and it beats closing.
  • Exit test (long run) - stay in the market only if price ≥ average total cost — in the long run you can escape every cost.

Profit (revenue vs total cost) is a separate question. A firm can lose money and still be right to keep producing right now.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

A firm sells at a price of 75. At its best output, its average variable cost is 60 and its average total cost is 80. What should it do in the short run and in the long run? Then suppose the price rises to 85 (same costs) — now what?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

Short-run shutdown test: produce if price ≥ average variable cost. Long-run exit test: stay only if price ≥ average total cost.
1
Price 75 — shutdown test.
75 ≥ 60 (AVC), so PRODUCE in the short run.
2
Price 75 — exit test.
75 < 80 (ATC), so it loses money → EXIT in the long run.
3
Price 85 — shutdown test.
85 ≥ 60, so produce.
4
Price 85 — exit test.
85 ≥ 80, so it covers all costs → stay in BOTH runs.
✓ Answer: At 75: produce short run, exit long run. At 85: produce in both runs.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

Price is 50, average variable cost is 55, average total cost is 70. Short run?
Shut down. 50 < 55 (AVC): it can't even cover variable cost, so producing loses more than closing.
A firm is losing money but price is above its average variable cost. Keep producing short run?
Yes. Losing money (TR < TC) is the profit test; the shutdown test (P vs AVC) says keep going for now.
