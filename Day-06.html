Short-Run Supply & the Shutdown Decision

Two different questions: should I produce right now, and should I stay in the market at all.

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

A food truck is losing money this month. Its pitch fee is already paid, whatever it does. Should it open tomorrow, or stay shut?

Decide, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Shutdown test (short run) — produce now if price ≥ average variable cost. Fixed costs are already spent, so covering variable cost beats closing.
  • Exit test (long run) — stay in the market only if price ≥ average total cost; in the long run you can escape every cost.

Profit (revenue vs total cost) is a separate question. A firm can lose money and still be right to keep producing right now.

🎯 Three decisions, three tests

🎧 Listen to this section

A competitive firm faces three separate questions. Keep them apart and the exam questions fall out cleanly.

1) How much to make → keep producing each unit while its MC ≤ price; stop when MC passes the price. 2) Produce at all, short run? → yes if price ≥ AVC. 3) Stay in the market, long run? → yes if price ≥ ATC.

Why these are the cut-offs

In the short run, the fixed cost is already spent — sunk. So the only thing to cover by opening is the variable cost. If the price clears AVC, opening loses less (or earns more) than closing. In the long run nothing is sunk; the firm only stays if the price covers every cost — that's ATC.

The trap Ifat-proofing: "losing money" is the profit test (price vs ATC), and it is not the same as the shutdown test (price vs AVC). A firm can be losing money yet absolutely right to keep producing this month.

Three price bands: above the green line (P > min ATC) the firm profits and stays in both runs. Between the lines (min AVC ≤ P < min ATC) it loses money but still produces short run, then exits long run. Below the red line (P < min AVC) it shuts down now. The MC curve above AVC is the firm's short-run supply curve.

The price of tea is 75 per box. How many boxes, short run and long run?

1
How many to make? Produce while MC ≤ price.
Box 4's MC is 60 (≤ 75) → make it. Box 5's MC is 90 (> 75) → stop. So 4 boxes.
2
Short run — is price ≥ AVC at 4?
75 ≥ 30 → yes, produce in the short run.
3
Long run — is price ≥ ATC at 4?
ATC at 4 is 80, and 75 < 80 → she loses money → exit in the long run.
✓ Answer at price 75: make 4 boxes in the short run, and none in the long run.

Now you try — reveal only after you work it out

Same table, but the price rises to 85 per box. How many boxes short run and long run?
4 boxes in both runs. MC of box 5 is 90 > 85, so still 4 boxes. ATC at 4 is 80, and 85 ≥ 80 → she covers all costs → stays in the long run too.
Price 50, AVC at best output 55, ATC 70. Short run?
Shut down. 50 < 55 (AVC): she can't even cover variable cost, so producing loses more than closing.
