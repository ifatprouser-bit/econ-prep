Demand & Supply Curves

How one shopper's demand adds up into a whole market — and what makes a curve move.

⏸ Before you listen - guess first

A coffee shop raises its price by one shekel and sells fewer cups. Did its demand curve move — or did we just slide to a new point on the same curve?

Guess, then press play.

🎧 Listen ~5 min

Play the episode, then work through the question below.

  • Move ALONG the curve - caused by the good's OWN price changing (more expensive → people buy less).
  • SHIFT the whole curve - caused by something else — income, tastes, the price of related goods, the number of buyers.

Market demand (or supply) = add up every buyer's (or seller's) quantity at each price; equilibrium is where the two meet.

🔢 Worked example

Part 1 · The question

In town T there are two groups of buyers. Group A has 7 buyers who each want 80 cakes at a price of 175. Group B has 8 buyers who each want 50 cakes at a price of 175. What is the market quantity demanded at a price of 175?

Part 2 · Solve it step by step

Market demand at a price = add up the quantity every buyer wants at that price. With identical groups, multiply each group's individual quantity by the number of buyers, then add.
1
Group A total.
7 buyers × 80 = 560
2
Group B total.
8 buyers × 50 = 400
3
Add the groups.
560 + 400 = 960 cakes
4
That is market demand at 175.
Repeat at every price to trace the whole market demand curve.
✓ Answer: 960 cakes are demanded at a price of 175.

Now you try - reveal only after you work it out

At a price of 100, each of 7 group-A buyers wants 160 and each of 8 group-B buyers wants 100. Market quantity demanded?
1,920. Group A: 7 × 160 = 1,120. Group B: 8 × 100 = 800. Total = 1,120 + 800 = 1,920.
Buyers' incomes rise (the good is normal). Does the demand curve shift or do we slide along it?
It SHIFTS right — income is not the good's own price. At every price, people now want more.
