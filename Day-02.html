Demand, Supply & Equilibrium

How one shopper's demand adds up into a whole market, where the market price comes from, and what makes a curve move.

⏸ Before you listen — guess first

A coffee shop raises its price by one shekel and sells fewer cups. Did its demand curve move — or did we just slide to a new point on the same curve?

Guess, then press play.

🎧 Start here — today's plan ~1 min

A quick map of what you'll learn today. Each section below also has its own short audio you can listen to while you read.

  • Move ALONG the curve — caused by the good's OWN price changing.
  • SHIFT the whole curve — caused by something else: income, tastes, related-good prices, number of buyers/sellers.

Market demand (or supply) = add up every buyer's (or seller's) quantity at each price. Equilibrium = the one price where the two meet.

🧳 Why we even build a "market" curve

🎧 Listen to this section

A town doesn't have one buyer — it has many, and each one wants a different amount. So how does the town land on one price that everybody pays?

The exam answers this with a table: it gives you what one buyer in each group wants at each price, and what one seller offers. Your job is to scale each up to the whole town, then find the single price where total wanted = total offered. That price is the equilibrium — the market price. That's the whole point of building the curves: to find where the town clears.

↔ Move along the curve vs. shift the whole curve

🎧 Listen to this section

This is the distinction the exam loves. The trick is one question:

Ask: did the good's OWN price change?
· Yes → move ALONG the same curve (just slide to a new point).
· No, something else changed → SHIFT the whole curve.
Left: the price rose, so we slide up the same curve — fewer bought. Right: incomes rose, so the whole curve moves out — more bought at every price.

➡️ Which way does it shift — right or left?

🎧 Listen to this section

Your memory hook: More → Right, Less → Left. "Right" means the curve moves outward, so there's a bigger quantity at every price.

Same logic both sides: more demand / easier-cheaper supply pushes the curve right; less demand / harder-costlier supply pulls it left.

What pushes DEMAND right (people want more at every price)

Income rises (for a normal good) · the good gets more popular / stronger taste · more buyers · a substitute gets pricier · a complement gets cheaper. The opposite of any of these pushes demand left.

What pushes SUPPLY right (sellers offer more at every price)

Input costs fall · better technology · more sellers. The opposite — input costs rise, fewer sellers — pushes supply left.

Then read the effect on price & quantity

What shiftsPriceQuantity
Demand → right↑ up↑ up
Demand → left↓ down↓ down
Supply → right↓ down↑ up
Supply → left↑ up↓ down

Pattern: demand shifts move price and quantity the same way. Supply shifts move them opposite ways.

🔧 Worked shift — a new factor

Bakers in a town adopt a faster oven that lowers their cost to make cakes. What happens?

Cost fell → it's easier/cheaper to produce → supply shifts right. From the table: price ↓ down, quantity ↑ up. More cakes are sold, and they're cheaper.

Now you try: buyers' incomes rise and cake is a normal good. Which curve shifts, which way, and what happens to price and quantity? (Reveal only after you answer.)
Income is not the good's own price → it SHIFTS the curve. Higher income → people want more → demand shifts right → price ↑ up, quantity ↑ up.
